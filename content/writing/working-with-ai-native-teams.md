That’s a great question, and one that’s getting louder as design tools become more accessible and AI closes the gap further. Here’s how I’d think about it:

**What engineers can do now**

- Build functional UIs quickly with component libraries, Figma-to-code tools, and AI-assisted design
- Make reasonable layout and styling decisions, especially for internal tools or MVP-stage products
- Iterate faster without a handoff step

**What designers still do that engineers typically don’t**

- **Research and problem framing.** Deciding _what_ to build and _why_ — user interviews, journey mapping, identifying the right problem. Most engineering training doesn’t emphasize this.
- **Systems thinking at the interaction level.** Not just “does this screen work?” but “does this flow make sense across 14 edge cases, error states, onboarding, and re-engagement?”
- **Visual craft and brand coherence.** Engineers can make things look clean. Designers make things feel _intentional_ — typography hierarchies, motion design, spatial rhythm, emotional tone.
- **Advocacy for the user.** In most orgs, engineers are evaluated on shipping and technical quality. Designers are evaluated on whether the thing actually works for people. That structural difference matters.

**The honest middle ground**

The question isn’t really “do we need designers?” — it’s “at what stage and scale do you need dedicated design expertise?” A two-person startup where the engineer has good taste? Probably fine without a designer for a while. A product with millions of users, complex workflows, and accessibility requirements? You’d feel the absence fast.

The more interesting shift is that the _boundary_ between the roles is blurring. The best teams aren’t asking “designer or engineer?” — they’re asking “who on this team has the skill to make this decision well, regardless of title?”

So: engineers who can design raise the floor. Designers raise the ceiling. Whether you need both depends on how high you’re trying to build.
